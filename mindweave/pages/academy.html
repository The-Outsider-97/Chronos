Foundations of the Weaver Discipline

The Mindweave protocol is built upon three pillars: metacognition, fluid intelligence (IQ), and emotional intelligence (EQ). Each corresponds to a distinct set of trainable competencies that, when developed together, enhance adaptive problem‑solving, interpersonal effectiveness, and self‑regulated learning. Below we explore the scientific underpinnings of each pillar and explain how they are woven into the campaign’s mechanics.

1. Metacognition

Definition: Metacognition refers to the awareness and control of one’s own cognitive processes. It is often summarised as “thinking about thinking” and comprises three essential skills:

  • Planning – Setting goals, allocating attentional resources, and selecting strategies before engaging in a task.
  • Monitoring – Self‑checking during task execution to assess progress and detect errors.
  • Evaluating – Reflecting on outcomes, identifying what worked, and adjusting future approaches.

Why it matters: High metacognitive ability is associated with academic achievement, effective problem‑solving, and transfer of learning to novel contexts (Veenman et al., 2006). In Mindweave, the debrief phase explicitly trains metacognition by requiring you to articulate your strategy, describe a moment of adaptation, and commit to real‑world transfer.

Key references:
  • Flavell, J. H. (1979). Metacognition and cognitive monitoring: A new area of cognitive–developmental inquiry. American Psychologist, 34(10), 906–911.
  • Veenman, M. V. J., Van Hout‑Wolters, B. H. A. M., & Afflerbach, P. (2006). Metacognition and learning: Conceptual and methodological considerations. Metacognition and Learning, 1(1), 3–14.

2. Fluid Intelligence (IQ)

Definition: Fluid intelligence (Gf) is the capacity to solve novel problems, identify patterns, and manipulate abstract information independently of acquired knowledge (Cattell, 1963). Core components include:

  • Working memory – The ability to hold and manipulate information over short periods.
  • Reasoning – Inductive and deductive logic, rule discovery.
  • Processing speed – The rate at which mental operations can be executed.

Trainability: A landmark study by Jaeggi and colleagues (2008) demonstrated that adaptive dual n‑back training can lead to significant gains in fluid intelligence, with effects transferring to untrained reasoning tasks. Subsequent meta‑analyses (Au et al., 2015) confirm small but reliable far‑transfer effects when training is adaptive and sufficiently prolonged.

Mindweave implementation: The IQ phase incorporates dual n‑back, resource management (planning under constraints), logic gate repair (deductive reasoning), task‑switching (cognitive flexibility), and threat‑code puzzles (mathematical reasoning). Each task is designed to load working memory and require rule‑based reasoning, promoting the kind of cognitive engagement linked to transfer.

Key references:
  • Cattell, R. B. (1963). Theory of fluid and crystallized intelligence: A critical experiment. Journal of Educational Psychology, 54(1), 1–22.
  • Jaeggi, S. M., Buschkuehl, M., Jonides, J., & Perrig, W. J. (2008). Improving fluid intelligence with training on working memory. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 105(19), 6829–6833.
  • Au, J., Sheehan, E., Tsai, N., Duncan, G. J., Buschkuehl, M., & Jaeggi, S. M. (2015). Improving fluid intelligence with training on working memory: a meta‑analysis. Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 22(2), 366–377.

3. Emotional Intelligence (EQ)

Definition: Emotional intelligence is the ability to perceive, use, understand, and manage emotions in oneself and others (Mayer & Salovey, 1997). The widely adopted four‑branch model includes:

  • Perceiving emotion – Recognising emotions in faces, voices, and body language.
  • Using emotion – Harnessing feelings to facilitate cognitive activities (e.g., creative thinking).
  • Understanding emotion – Comprehending emotional language and how emotions combine/progress.
  • Managing emotion – Regulating one’s own and others’ emotions to achieve goals.

Evidence base: Higher EQ predicts better mental health, job performance, leadership effectiveness, and relationship satisfaction (O’Boyle et al., 2011). Training studies (e.g., Nelis et al., 2009) show that emotional competencies can be improved through structured programmes involving psycho‑education, role‑play, and real‑world practice.

Mindweave implementation: The EQ phase targets all four branches:

  • Perceiving – Micro‑expression analysis based on the Facial Action Coding System (FACS).
  • Using/Understanding – Empathy bridge dialogues that require validating and reflecting emotions.
  • Managing – Cognitive reappraisal (reframing stressful statements) and co‑regulation boundary setting.
  • Regulation – Biometric pulse matching to synchronise with the Architect’s emotional state.
Key references:
  • Mayer, J. D., & Salovey, P. (1997). What is emotional intelligence? In P. Salovey & D. Sluyter (Eds.), Emotional development and emotional intelligence (pp. 3–31). Basic Books.
  • Nelis, D., Quoidbach, J., Mikolajczak, M., & Hansenne, M. (2009). Increasing emotional intelligence: (How) is it possible? Personality and Individual Differences, 47(1), 36–41.
  • O’Boyle, E. H., Humphrey, R. H., Pollack, J. M., Hawver, T. H., & Story, P. A. (2011). The relation between emotional intelligence and job performance: A meta‑analysis. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 32(5), 788–818.

Why Train Metacognition, IQ, and EQ Together?

Real‑world challenges rarely isolate cognitive from emotional demands. A leader must analyse data (IQ), regulate their own frustration (EQ), and reflect on their decision‑making process (metacognition). Neuroimaging studies show that executive functions (IQ) and emotion regulation (EQ) share overlapping prefrontal circuits (Ochsner & Gross, 2005), suggesting that integrated training may yield synergistic gains. Mindweave’s phased campaign deliberately interweaves these domains, culminating in a debrief that cements transferable learning.

Reference:

Ochsner, K. N., & Gross, J. J. (2005). The cognitive control of emotion. Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 9(5), 242–249.
